## Summary

The eviction literature has bifurcated into two camps that IronCache must reconcile. The first camp optimizes hit ratio with sophisticated recency/frequency hybrids: ARC (FAST'03) self-tunes a parameter p that splits a 2c-entry directory into recency (T1/B1) and frequency (T2/B2) lists, keeps no frequency counts, and "substantially outperforms LRU" while being parameter-free; LIRS (SIGMETRICS'02) uses inter-reference recency to get scan resistance; 2Q, MQ, CAR (the CLOCK variant of ARC), and CLOCK-Pro round out this family. W-TinyLFU (Caffeine) is the strongest of these for in-memory KV: a ~1% LRU admission window feeds a ~99% SLRU main region, gated by a frequency-based admission filter built on a 4-bit Count-Min Sketch (~8 bytes/entry) with a doorkeeper and periodic halving (aging) of all counters; Caffeine adds hill-climbing to adapt the window size. These deliver excellent hit ratios but pay a structural tax: every cache HIT mutates a doubly-linked list under a lock (eager promotion), which caps multicore throughput.

The second, newer camp (the one most aligned with IronCache's "max throughput per core" thesis) abandons LRU's per-hit list surgery in favor of lazy promotion + quick demotion on FIFO queues. S3-FIFO (SOSP'23) uses three fixed-size FIFOs — a 10% small probationary queue, a 90% main queue, and a ghost queue (object IDs only) — with a 2-bit frequency counter capped at 3. On eviction from the small queue, objects with freq>1 are promoted to main; freq≤1 are demoted to ghost; main-queue eviction reinserts-with-decrement if freq>0. The insight: the median one-hit-wonder ratio jumps to 72% when measured over short sequences (first 10% of unique objects), so a small FIFO filters them cheaply. S3-FIFO had the lowest mean miss ratio on 10/14 datasets and top-3 on 13/14 across 6594 traces / 856B requests, with ~6x higher throughput than highly-optimized LRU at 16 cores. SIEVE (NSDI'24) is even simpler: one FIFO + a "hand" pointer that scans tail→head, clearing per-object visited bits and evicting the first unvisited object (a hit just sets the bit, no list move). SIEVE needs only 12-21 lines to retrofit into existing caches (C++, Go, Rust, etc.), is ~16% faster single-threaded and >2x faster at 16 threads than optimized LRU, and has the lowest miss ratio on >45% of web traces (next best: 15%).

For IronCache the concurrency story is decisive. LRU/LFU/ARC/W-TinyLFU all require taking a lock (or doing CAS-heavy list splicing) on the hot read path; under Redis-like single-shard-per-core or sharded designs this is the throughput ceiling. FIFO-based policies need only an atomic flag set on hit, enabling lock-free or near-lock-free reads. SIEVE and S3-FIFO match or beat W-TinyLFU on web/KV hit ratio while being radically more scalable, which is exactly IronCache's goal. The catch: neither maintains the stack property — they are non-monotonic (a bigger cache can, pathologically, give a worse miss ratio), SIEVE has no ghost list so it cannot quickly re-admit a recently-evicted hot object and degrades at very small cache sizes and on scan-heavy block workloads. S3-FIFO's ghost queue mitigates this. SIEVE's middle-of-list eviction also breaks the simple circular-array implementation, requiring a real linked list (still O(1) per op, but pointer-chasing).

Learned/adaptive policies (LeCaR, CACHEUS, LRB, LHD, Hyperbolic, GDSF) are mostly out of scope for an in-memory KV hot path. LeCaR (HotStorage'18) uses regret-minimization over LRU+LFU experts and beats ARC by up to 18x at tiny cache sizes; CACHEUS (FAST'21) generalizes this with SR-LRU and CR-LFU experts. LRB (NSDI'20) approximates Belady's MIN with gradient-boosted trees over per-object feature vectors, cutting CDN WAN/byte traffic ~4-25%, but its per-object feature tracking and model inference are far too heavy for nanosecond-scale in-memory lookups. LHD (NSDI'18) ranks objects by expected hits-per-space via online conditional-probability estimation. IronCache should treat these as research inspiration for an optional, AI-driven offline/background tuner (e.g., choosing window sizes or admission thresholds), not as the primary online policy. Recommendation: ship a SIEVE-or-S3-FIFO-class FIFO policy as the default for lock-friendliness, keep an optional W-TinyLFU-style frequency admission filter (CM-sketch + doorkeeper) for frequency-skewed workloads, expose a ghost queue for re-admission, and reserve learned methods for a background "auto-tuner" service.

## Mechanisms: borrow, adapt, or reject

| Mechanism | System | Stance | What it does | Rationale for IronCache |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| SIEVE (FIFO + hand + visited bit) | SIEVE (NSDI'24) | **borrow** | Single FIFO list with a roving hand pointer; a hit sets a 1-bit visited flag (no list move), eviction scans clearing flags and removes the first unvisited object. Lazy promotion + quick demotion. | Best concurrency-to-hit-ratio tradeoff in the literature: hit path is a single atomic bit set (lock-free reads), 12-21 LOC, >2x LRU throughput at 16 threads and lowest miss ratio on >45% of web traces. This is the closest match to IronCache's 'max throughput per core' thesis. Ship as a default policy. |
| S3-FIFO (three static FIFOs + 2-bit freq + ghost) | S3-FIFO (SOSP'23) | **borrow** | 10% small probationary FIFO filters one-hit-wonders, 90% main FIFO holds promoted objects, ghost FIFO (IDs only) enables fast re-admission; 2-bit freq capped at 3 drives promotion/reinsertion. | Adds a ghost queue (which SIEVE lacks) for quick re-admission of recently-evicted hot keys, fixing SIEVE's small-cache weakness, while staying FIFO-friendly (~6x LRU throughput at 16 cores) and winning on 10/14 datasets. Strong default/alternative; the small-FIFO filter also reduces churn from scans. |
| W-TinyLFU frequency admission (CM-sketch + doorkeeper + aging) | Caffeine / TinyLFU | **adapt** | 4-bit Count-Min Sketch (~8 B/entry) estimates frequency; doorkeeper bloom filter ignores one-hit items; periodic counter halving ages the sketch; admission compares window victim vs main victim frequency. | Keep the CM-sketch+doorkeeper as an OPTIONAL admission filter in front of a FIFO main policy (frequency-skewed workloads), but reject the LRU window + SLRU main + hill-climbing scaffolding because the per-hit SLRU promotion reintroduces lock contention. Use the sketch only at admission/eviction, not on every read. |
| ARC self-tuning recency/frequency balance | ARC (FAST'03) | **adapt** | Four lists (T1/B1 recency, T2/B2 frequency) with a self-tuned parameter p (target \|T1\|) and ~2c ghost directory; parameter-free, no frequency counts. | Borrow the self-tuning idea (adapt the split between recency and frequency from ghost-hit feedback) and the ghost-directory concept, but reject the four-list LRU implementation: every hit moves an entry between lists under a lock, and it doubles metadata. Patent history (IBM) is also a caution for verbatim implementation. |
| CLOCK / second-chance | Classic (CLOCK-Pro, CAR) | **adapt** | Circular buffer with reference bits approximating LRU at O(1) and low metadata; hit just sets a bit. | CLOCK's per-hit 'set a bit' is exactly the lazy-promotion primitive SIEVE generalizes; treat SIEVE as the modern, scan-resistant CLOCK and adopt that lineage rather than vanilla CLOCK (which is not scan-resistant and lacks quick demotion). |
| Approximate sampled LRU/LFU | Redis maxmemory-policy | **adapt** | Samples N keys (default 5) and evicts the approximate LRU/LFU victim, avoiding global ordering structures. | For Redis-wire compatibility IronCache must expose allkeys-lru/lfu/random/volatile-* policy NAMES, but should back them with O(1) FIFO-class internals rather than Redis's sampling. Sampling is a reasonable fallback for the LFU mode where exact recency ordering is not required. |
| LIRS / inter-reference recency | LIRS (SIGMETRICS'02) | **reject** | Classifies blocks by IRR to get scan/loop resistance superior to LRU at LRU-like cost. | Scan resistance is valuable but LIRS's stack pruning and HIR/LIR bookkeeping are complex and lock-heavy on the hot path; S3-FIFO's small-FIFO filter and SIEVE's visited-bit achieve adequate scan resistance far more cheaply for an in-memory KV cache. |
| LeCaR / CACHEUS expert ensembles | LeCaR (HotStorage'18), CACHEUS (FAST'21) | **reject** | Online regret-minimization / adaptive weighting over expert policies (LRU, LFU, SR-LRU, CR-LFU) to track shifting workloads. | Running multiple shadow simulators and reweighting on every request is too expensive for nanosecond-scale lookups and complicates the lock-free path. The ADAPTIVITY idea is worth keeping, but realized as a slow background tuner that switches/parameterizes a single fast policy, not as an online per-request ensemble. |
| LRB (Learning Relaxed Belady, GBDT) | LRB (NSDI'20) | **reject** | Gradient-boosted trees predict each object's relaxed-Belady eviction value from feature vectors to approximate the MIN oracle. | Per-object feature collection plus tree inference per eviction is appropriate for CDN/flash byte-miss optimization, not for a memory-bandwidth-bound in-memory cache. Keep as inspiration for an optional offline 'Belady-distance' analysis to benchmark how far IronCache's online policy is from optimal. |
| LHD / Hyperbolic / GDSF (size & cost aware) | LHD (NSDI'18), Hyperbolic (ATC'17), GDSF | **reject** | Rank objects by expected hit density / hyperbolic priority / greedy-dual size-frequency-cost to handle variable object sizes and miss costs. | Size-aware value functions matter most for variable-size web/CDN objects; for a Redis-style KV cache object sizes vary less and per-eviction floating-point scoring hurts throughput. Revisit only if IronCache targets large-value or tiered (compressed/SSD) storage where size-cost tradeoffs dominate. |

## Implications for IronCache

- Default eviction policy should be a FIFO-class lazy-promotion / quick-demotion algorithm (SIEVE for simplicity, S3-FIFO when a re-admission ghost is wanted) so the read hot path is a single atomic visited-bit set, enabling lock-free or sharded-lock reads and maximizing throughput per core.
- Add an optional S3-FIFO-style ghost queue (object keys/hashes only, not values) to recover SIEVE's main weakness — inability to quickly re-admit a recently-evicted hot key and poor behavior at very small cache sizes.
- Offer a W-TinyLFU-style frequency admission filter as a pluggable front-end (4-bit Count-Min Sketch ~8 B/entry + doorkeeper bloom filter + periodic counter halving) for frequency-skewed workloads, but invoke the sketch only at admission/eviction, never on every read, to preserve the lock-free read path.
- Because FIFO-based policies are non-monotonic (no stack property), do NOT rely on miss-ratio-curve / hit-ratio-curve assumptions for capacity planning; instead expose live hit-ratio counters and a shadow-cache sampler for sizing.
- Implement SIEVE with a real intrusive doubly-linked list (not a circular array) because eviction removes from the middle; keep per-entry overhead tiny (1 visited bit packed into existing flag bits, 2 pointers). S3-FIFO needs a 2-bit counter + ghost-entry hash set.
- Maintain Redis-wire compatibility by exposing the standard policy names (noeviction, allkeys-lru, allkeys-lfu, allkeys-random, volatile-ttl, volatile-lru, volatile-lfu, volatile-random) as configuration aliases that map onto IronCache's superior internal FIFO-class engine, rather than reimplementing Redis's sampled approximation.
- Reserve learned/adaptive methods (LeCaR/CACHEUS-style switching, LRB-style Belady approximation) for an optional background 'auto-tuner' that periodically retunes a single fast policy's knobs (window %, freq threshold, ghost size) from sampled traces — never an online per-request ensemble.
- Provide an offline Belady-MIN (and sampled-Belady) analysis tool so IronCache can report how close its online policy is to optimal on a customer's trace, turning 'most efficient cache' into a measurable, defensible claim.
- Design the eviction policy as a trait/interface from day one (admit(), on_hit(), evict_candidate()) so SIEVE, S3-FIFO, and W-TinyLFU can be swapped and benchmarked on identical traces; pick the default empirically from a standard trace corpus (the 6594-trace / cachemon corpus).

## Research papers and primary sources

- **FIFO Queues are All You Need for Cache Eviction (S3-FIFO)** (SOSP 2023). Three static FIFO queues (10% small + 90% main + ghost) with a 2-bit freq counter cap 3 implement quick demotion of one-hit-wonders; lowest mean miss ratio on 10/14 datasets and ~6x LRU throughput at 16 cores. [source](https://www.cs.cmu.edu/~rvinayak/papers/s3-fifo-sosp-2023-fifo-queues-are-all-you-need-for-cache-eviction.pdf) Relevance: Primary candidate for IronCache's default policy; the ghost queue and small-FIFO filter are directly adoptable for a lock-friendly, scan-resistant cache.
- **SIEVE is Simpler than LRU: an Efficient Turn-Key Eviction Algorithm for Web Caches** (NSDI 2024). One FIFO + hand pointer + per-object visited bit; hits set a bit (no list move), eviction scans for an unvisited object. 12-21 LOC to integrate, >2x LRU throughput at 16 threads, lowest miss ratio on >45% of web traces. [source](https://junchengyang.com/publication/nsdi24-SIEVE.pdf) Relevance: Strongest match to IronCache's throughput-per-core goal; minimal metadata and lock-free reads. Note: no ghost list, non-stack-property, weak at very small caches / scans.
- **TinyLFU: A Highly Efficient Cache Admission Policy** (ACM ToS 2017 (Einziger, Friedman, Manes)). Frequency-based admission via a 4-bit Count-Min Sketch with a doorkeeper bloom filter and periodic counter halving (aging); admits only items whose estimated frequency beats the eviction victim. [source](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1512.00727) Relevance: Source of the optional frequency-admission filter for IronCache; the sketch+doorkeeper+aging design is the part worth borrowing (not the per-hit LRU/SLRU window).
- **ARC: A Self-Tuning, Low Overhead Replacement Cache** (USENIX FAST 2003). Self-tunes parameter p (target T1 size) to balance recency vs frequency using ghost-list hits, parameter-free, no frequency counts, ~2c directory; substantially outperforms LRU. [source](https://www2.cs.uh.edu/~paris/7360/PAPERS03/arcfast.pdf) Relevance: Reference for self-tuning and ghost-feedback adaptivity; rejected for hot-path implementation (lock-heavy four-list LRU, 2x metadata, patent history).
- **LIRS: An Efficient Low Inter-reference Recency Set Replacement Policy** (ACM SIGMETRICS 2002). Uses Inter-Reference Recency (distance between last two references) to classify hot/cold blocks, giving scan/loop resistance at near-LRU cost. [source](https://www.openu.ac.il/home/wiseman/2os/lru/lirs.pdf) Relevance: Benchmark for scan resistance; informs what FIFO-class policies must match, but its bookkeeping is too heavy for IronCache's hot path.
- **Driving Cache Replacement with ML-based LeCaR** (USENIX HotStorage 2018). Regret-minimization online learning over two experts (LRU, LFU) beats ARC by >18x at small cache sizes. [source](https://www.usenix.org/conference/hotstorage18/presentation/vietri) Relevance: Inspiration for an adaptive auto-tuner; rejected as an online per-request mechanism due to cost.
- **Learning Cache Replacement with CACHEUS** (USENIX FAST 2021). Adaptive expert ensemble (incl. new SR-LRU and CR-LFU) targeting four workload primitives: LFU-friendly, LRU-friendly, scan, churn. [source](https://www.usenix.org/system/files/fast21-rodriguez.pdf) Relevance: Defines workload-primitive taxonomy IronCache can use to design its trace test-suite and auto-tuner switching logic.
- **Learning Relaxed Belady for Content Distribution Network Caching (LRB)** (USENIX NSDI 2020). Gradient-boosted trees approximate Belady's MIN via a 'relaxed Belady' objective over per-object features; ~4-25% WAN/byte-traffic reduction for CDNs. [source](https://www.usenix.org/conference/nsdi20/presentation/song) Relevance: Inspiration for an offline Belady-gap benchmark; rejected for online in-memory use (feature tracking + inference too heavy).
- **LHD: Improving Cache Hit Rate by Maximizing Hit Density** (USENIX NSDI 2018). Evicts the object with least expected hits-per-space-time (hit density), estimated online by conditional probability over object age/class. [source](https://www.usenix.org/system/files/conference/nsdi18/nsdi18-beckmann.pdf) Relevance: Size/cost-aware value-function reference for a future tiered or large-value IronCache mode; not for the default KV hot path.
- **Can Increasing the Hit Ratio Hurt Cache Throughput? (Long Version)** (arXiv 2024 (Yang et al.)). Quantifies the tension between higher hit ratio and higher throughput, showing complex high-hit-ratio policies can reduce overall system throughput. [source](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2404.16219) Relevance: Directly supports IronCache's bias toward simple FIFO-class policies: argues efficiency must be measured as end-to-end throughput, not hit ratio alone.

## Open questions

- Which default policy wins on a representative Redis/KV workload corpus once value sizes, TTLs, and write ratios are included — SIEVE, S3-FIFO, or W-TinyLFU-fronted FIFO? Needs a head-to-head benchmark on the cachemon 6594-trace corpus plus KV-specific traces.
- How should eviction interact with TTL expiry and with active-expiration sweeps (Redis volatile-* semantics) without adding a second lock on the hot path?
- What is the right ghost-queue sizing and representation (full key vs 8-byte hash) to balance re-admission accuracy against memory in a 'minimal memory' cache, and what false-positive rate is acceptable?
- Under a sharded single-thread-per-core architecture, is a per-shard SIEVE list sufficient, or is a global frequency sketch needed for cross-shard hot-key detection — and how is the sketch shared without contention?
- How do FIFO-class policies behave under adversarial / scan / churn workloads compared to LIRS/ARC, and do we need an explicit scan-detection guard beyond the small-FIFO filter?
- Can an offline Belady-MIN gap be turned into a continuously-reported online metric cheaply enough to ship as a product feature?
- Does the non-stack (non-monotonic) property of FIFO-class policies cause user-visible surprises during cache resizing / autoscaling, and how do we document/guard against it?
- For the optional AI auto-tuner, what is the minimal set of tunables (window %, freq threshold, ghost size, small/main split) and what cadence/feedback signal (sampled hit ratio) makes retuning safe and stable?
